Cognitive Biases in Financial Decision-Making

Theoretical Development Timeline: 1970s to Present

From Rational Models to Behavioral Economics

This timeline traces the evolution of our understanding of cognitive biases in financial decision-making, from Kahneman and Tversky's pioneering work to contemporary applications in Indian financial markets.

Foundation Era: 1970s-1980s
1974
Heuristics and Biases
Kahneman & Tversky
Anchoring Availability Representativeness
Published seminal paper in Science demonstrating that people rely on heuristics (mental shortcuts) that lead to systematic biases. Introduced three key heuristics: anchoring (first number influences judgment), availability (recent events seem more likely), and representativeness (judging probability by similarity to stereotypes).

Overreaction Hypothesis
De Bondt & Thaler
Market Inefficiency Contrarian Strategy
Demonstrated that stock markets systematically overreact to dramatic news (both good and bad), leading to predictable reversals. Past "losers" outperform past "winners" over 3-5 year horizons, contradicting efficient market hypothesis. This opened door for behavioral explanations of market anomalies.

Expansion Era: 1990s-2000s
1991
Myopic Loss Aversion
Benartzi & Thaler
Equity Premium Puzzle Investment Horizon
Explained why stocks command high returns (equity premium puzzle): loss aversion + frequent evaluation = pain from short-term volatility. Investors who check portfolios daily experience more "losses" than those who check annually, even with identical returns. This discourages equity investment despite superior long-term returns.

Explains India's low equity market participation (~2-3% population) despite high returns. Zerodha/Groww apps' daily notifications may increase myopic loss aversion. SIPs work by reducing evaluation frequency.

Mathematical model showing how rational individuals create irrational group outcomes. When people observe others' actions and ignore their own private information, cascades form. These are fragileâ€”small shocks can reverse entire cascades. Explains bank runs, market bubbles, and fashion trends.

Do Investors Trade Too Much?
Terrance Odean
Overconfidence Trading Volume
Analyzed 10,000 brokerage accounts showing excessive trading reduces returns by ~4% annually due to transaction costs and poor timing. Men trade 45% more than women and have correspondingly lower returns. Overconfidence (illusion of knowledge and control) drives excessive trading despite evidence that passive strategies outperform.

Thaler & Benartzi
Applied Behavioral Economics Policy Design
Practical application of behavioral insights: employees commit to increase savings from FUTURE raises (not current income). Avoids loss aversion pain. Increased savings rates from 3.5% to 13.6% over 40 months. Demonstrated that understanding biases enables better policy design without mandates or financial incentives.

Contemporary Era: 2010s-Present
2011
Thinking, Fast and Slow
Daniel Kahneman
System 1 & 2 Synthesis
Magnum opus synthesizing 40 years of research into accessible framework. System 1 (fast, intuitive, emotional) vs. System 2 (slow, deliberate, logical). Most financial decisions use System 1 by default, leading to biases. System 2 is "lazy" and only engaged when necessary. Understanding this duality is key to debiasing.

System 1 drives festival season impulse spending. Insurance buying decisions often System 1 (agent pressure) vs. System 2 (needs analysis). SIP investments work by making System 1 do the right thing automatically.

Research Opportunities in Indian Context

  • Cultural variations in loss aversion: Does Indian collectivism amplify or reduce loss aversion compared to Western individualism?
  • Digital payment biases: How do UPI and digital wallets affect mental accounting and spending behavior?
  • Gender and financial decision-making: Indian women show different investment patternsâ€”overconfidence gap, risk tolerance differences
  • Herding in IPO markets: Systematic study of cascade formation in Indian IPO oversubscriptions (Zomato, Nykaa, Paytm)
  • Demonetization natural experiment: How did sudden cash withdrawal affect mental accounting and spending patterns?
  • Religious festivals and consumption: Mental accounting around Diwali, Eid, Christmas spending vs. regular months
  • Crypto adoption in India: Social cascade analysis, FOMO measurement, regulatory impact on bias activation
  • Financial literacy interventions: Which debiasing strategies work in Indian educational context?
  • Nudge design for retirement savings: Optimal defaults for EPF, NPS in Indian workplace settings
  • Algorithm aversion in robo-advisory: Cross-cultural comparison of AI trust in financial decisions
